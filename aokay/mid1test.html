MID-1

Manual Input Device

There's never been a computer peripheral quite like this.

Introducing the MID-1, a 40% mechanical keyboard designed from the ground up with an architectural sensibility. It’s like the weird house that one artsy friend lives in—painted black, boxy, conspicuously lacking certain modern conveniences, and lots of dimmer switches. It’s clearly not for everyone, yet you can’t let it go.

Weird little thing, big story, let’s get into it.

Base kit pricing: $350

Availability

The MID-1 isn’t available to purchase just yet.

I’m currently producing a small pilot batch for early supporters. Once those orders are successfully fulfilled, I plan to run a wider public sale. That sale will follow a reservation-based, in-stock model: you’ll be able to reserve your spot during a reservation window for $10, then purchase a ready-to-ship unit once production wraps.

Think of it like a group buy—but without the upfront payment or the risk.

If you’d like to stay in the loop, you can [join the interest list] or follow along on Discord. In the meantime, I’ve put this page together so you can get a detailed look at what the MID-1 is and what it offers.

The story

Purpose-built, purposely built differently

This wasn’t designed to be minimal, flashy, or inspired by a sunrise. It was designed like a building—structure first. Bent stainless steel, visible fasteners, no decorative nonsense. You can see how it’s put together, and you can take it apart the same way. There’s some toolbox in its DNA. Some architecture. A nod to old desk lamps, too—the kind with heavy bases and indirect light that subtly illuminates. The MID-1’s lighting system borrows that idea: functional, atmospheric, a little industrial. It invites you to handle it, build it, live with it. Nothing is hidden, nothing is over-designed, and nothing is precious—just solid parts, chosen to last, assembled with intent.

Features

Sheet metal construction

The KB-40-A uses thin stainless steel to create a solid enclosure with less material. Conventional sheet metal shaping gives the board soft corners and crisp edges. The case is finished with a smooth, matte black powder coating.

Like typing on a cloud, if clouds were made of steel!

Simple but substantial

Weighing 985 g (2.17 lbs), the KB-40-A has the heft of a solid CNC aluminum board, but the delicate lines of folded sheet material.

Light-up buttons

For those who enjoy control panels: two illuminated buttons toggle functions like caps lock, audio mute, and layers. The buttons latch down and light up with a satisfying, tactile click.

Straightforward assembly

Exposed, accessible screws secure the PCB and case. Lifting the hood to access switches only requires the removal of four M3 screws located on the front and back.

Doesn't need a coffee break or a pep talk—it just gets to work!

Hotswap PCB

An original PCB design by A.Okay! featuring Kailh hotswap sockets, USB-C connectivity, pre-soldered buttons and rotary encoder, and an immersion gold finish. It's an unfussy, 1.6 mm-sturdy, one-piece circuit board: no flex cuts, daughter boards, or tiny cables.

Sounds like a keyboard

The heavy stainless steel gives the hollow case a substantial, shapable sound. Leave the interior open for resonance or add the included wool felt case padding or IXPE PCB foam to tone it down. There's room to experiment.

Here's how the board sounds with wool felt, IXPE foam, Gateron Baby Kangaroo 2.0 switches (tactile, long-pole), and MTNU keycaps.

Louder than your boss' complaints. And honestly, isn't that the dream?

Repairable

Composed entirely of readily-available, off-the-shelf components (save the case and PCB). If parts require replacement, we can either provide or tell you where to find them.

The A.Okay! lifetime promise

Listen up. As long as I’m around and you’re using it, I’ve got your back.

The MID-1 is built to last—but if anything breaks, I’ll make it right. Whether it’s a gasket, a foot, or one of the stainless steel case parts: if you can return it, I’ll replace it. Free of charge.

This doesn’t cover lost parts, or damage from, say, a fire—but if something wore out, cracked, or failed under normal use, that’s on me. I made it. And I’ll take care of it. You just have to send it, because I want to learn from the issue.

For everything else—lost parts, modding questions, weird lighting behavior, mystery rattles—just reach out. I’ll be here.
